The Greenhouse Effect


Once again, greenhouse gases are gases found in our atmosphere that can absorb infrared waves from the sun; these gases re-emit the infrared reflected from the earthâ€™s surface. Instead of allowing the infrared waves to pass and leave the atmosphere, greenhouse gases trap then re-emit half the heat to space and a half back to the earth. There are a few main GHGs, and here they are listed in order of magnitude: Carbon Dioxide CO2, Methane CH4, Nitrous Oxide N2O, Fluorinated gas derivatives YFx and Hydrofluorocarbons HFCs.



What allows for the absorption of infrared waves?


Vibrational Motion.


The degrees of freedom are ways in which a gaseous particle can displace itself in anyway; they are broken down into translational motion, rotational motion and vibrational motion. However, only vibrational motion is important concerning the absorption of infrared waves. Vibrational motion explains that molecules tend to vibrate as energy is supplied to them, this causes periodic disturbances between bonded atoms and their centers of mass; in our case, vibrational motion allows certain gaseous molecules to interact with infrared waves. To simplify, carbon dioxide will be used as the model for GHGs. Although CO2 has a linear molecular geometry, it can nonetheless experience four different vibrational modes: asymmetric stretch, symmetric stretch and two bending. Each of these four modes, can absorb a certain wavelength 3 of which happen to be in the infrared range, this is why CO2 is notorious for its heating capabilities.



Why are three of four modes IR active in CO2?


We must first address how to find the number of vibrational modes in a molecule. The equation is 3N - 5 for linear molecules and 3N - 6 for non-linear, where N represents the number of atoms in a particular molecule. Generally speaking, only if a vibrational mode induces an artificial dipole will it absorb an IR photon. Therefore, symmetric stretching in CO2 is not IR active whereas the other modes are.




Bonds as springs


To understand this complex phenomenon, we must first address that bonds are not stationary; rather, they act like springs that can compress and expand when energy is supplied.

Think of this as pulling on one end of a spring with the other attached to a sturdy wall. When tugging, the spring absorbs the kinetic energy (movement) produced by your arms and converts it into elastic potential energy; the spring is now in a state of stress. When you let go of the spring, its potential energy converts back into kinetic and returns to its equilibrium position or where it would be at rest.

So when a carbon dioxide molecule makes contact with infrared waves that match one of three discrete values, it will absorb a photon as a spring would absorb Ek. The molecule is now in an excited state; it can now release its newly gained energy in a few ways:

  1. The photon is re-emitted in a fraction of a second upon absorption:
    • In the general up direction (space)
    • In the general down direction (earth)
  2. The gas molecule in its excited state collides with any other particle and transforms its energy into heat
